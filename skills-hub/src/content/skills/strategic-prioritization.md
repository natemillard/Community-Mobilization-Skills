## Definition

The practice of making intentional, transparent decisions about where to focus energy and resources — especially when conditions are imperfect, trade-offs are real, and multiple priorities compete for limited capacity.

The hardest truth in community mobilization is that you cannot do everything. Every yes to one priority is an implicit no to others. Strategic prioritization is the practice of making those trade-offs visible, involving the right people in making them, and being honest about what is being chosen and what is being set aside.

Program stewardship means taking responsibility for the long-term health of programs — not just their launch, but their sustainability, their quality, and their eventual transition to community ownership.

## Examples in Red Cross Work

- An ED facilitates a board and community advisory process to evaluate three potential priority areas using shared criteria — and presents the result with a clear explanation of what was chosen and why, including the trade-offs.
- A chapter team reviews a program that has been running for five years and acknowledges honestly that it is no longer serving the community it was designed for — and begins a deliberate transition rather than sustaining it out of inertia.
- An ED creates a simple dashboard of program health indicators — reach, quality, community feedback, staff capacity — and reviews it quarterly with community advisors, not just internally.

## This Skill in Everyday Life

A parent who has to choose which of three children's activities to support this semester — when there is only budget and time for two — is practicing strategic prioritization. The skill is in making the trade-off explicit, involving the children in the decision, and choosing based on articulated values rather than whoever argues loudest.

## Guiding Questions

1. What are you continuing to fund or staff that is no longer producing meaningful outcomes — and why?
2. Who is involved in making prioritization decisions — and who should be?
3. What trade-offs are you making implicitly that should be made explicitly?
4. How would you know if a program was ready to be handed to community ownership?

## Sub-Competencies

- **Analyze the priority-setting environment** — Identify the competing needs, capacity limits, and contextual pressures that affect what can be prioritized.
- **Facilitate a transparent and equitable prioritization process** — Help community partners apply shared criteria and articulate trade-offs.
- **Evaluate the effectiveness of the prioritization process** — Assess the quality of decisions made, equity of participation, and impact on trust.
- **Align resource allocation with community priorities** — Ensure spending and staffing decisions reflect the priorities the community has identified.
- **Develop transition and stewardship plans** — Design approaches for sustaining and eventually transferring programs based on what was learned.

## Proficiency Levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Understand It | I can explain why making trade-offs explicit matters more than trying to do everything. I understand what program stewardship means. |
| Can Do It | I have facilitated a prioritization process using shared criteria. I can evaluate program health across multiple dimensions, not just reach. |
| Do It Consistently | I regularly review program health with community advisors, not just internally. I name trade-offs explicitly in planning conversations. |
| Coach Others | I design prioritization processes for teams and help others see what is being implicitly chosen. I model transparent stewardship decision-making. |
