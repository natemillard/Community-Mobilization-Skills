## Definition

The practice of coordinating strategies across individual, community, organizational, and systems levels so that programs and partnerships reinforce each other — creating layered, sustainable impact rather than isolated efforts.

A food pantry that distributes groceries is working at one level. The same organization, partnering with a health clinic to provide nutrition education, connecting clients to workforce training, and advocating for grocery store siting in underserved areas — that is building across levels. The work at each level reinforces the work at every other level.

The Social Ecological Model provides the framework: individual, interpersonal, organizational, community, and systems. Building across levels means having a deliberate strategy at each level — and designing them so they connect.

## Examples in Red Cross Work

- A chapter designs a disaster preparedness strategy that includes individual household kits, neighborhood preparedness teams, organizational protocols for partner coordination, and advocacy for building code reform — and tracks progress at all four levels.
- An ED maps which existing programs are operating at which levels of the Social Ecological Model and identifies the levels where there is no strategy at all — and uses that gap analysis to guide the next year of planning.
- A chapter team brings together individual survivors, community organizations, public agencies, and a systems-change advocate in a single planning table — and designs meeting formats that allow each level to inform the others.

## This Skill in Everyday Life

A parent who helps their child develop individual coping skills, creates family routines that support those skills, connects the child to a supportive peer group, and advocates at the school board for better mental health resources — is building across levels. Every level they work at makes the others more effective.

## Guiding Questions

1. At which levels of the Social Ecological Model does your chapter currently have a strategy — and which are missing?
2. How are your individual-level programs connected to systems-level advocacy?
3. What would it look like for your community partners to coordinate across levels rather than work in parallel?
4. Where is your work producing isolated outcomes rather than reinforcing change?

## Sub-Competencies

- **Analyze a community challenge across multiple system levels** — Identify how individual, community, organizational, and systems factors interact to produce a challenge.
- **Identify leverage points for intervention** — Pinpoint where programs and partnerships can most effectively influence outcomes.
- **Design a coordinated multi-level intervention strategy** — Create a strategy that aligns programs and partnerships across levels.
- **Evaluate how effectively programs reinforce each other** — Assess whether existing programs are working in alignment and identify opportunities to improve coherence.
- **Articulate how layered strategy improves outcomes** — Explain clearly why a layered approach produces better results than isolated programs.

## Proficiency Levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Understand It | I can explain the Social Ecological Model and describe why working at multiple levels produces better outcomes than single-level programs. |
| Can Do It | I can map existing programs across the five levels of the Social Ecological Model and identify where gaps exist. |
| Do It Consistently | I design programs and partnerships with explicit connections across levels. I track progress at each level and use that data to adjust strategy. |
| Coach Others | I help teams apply the Social Ecological Model to their work. I facilitate cross-level planning processes and help partners see how their work connects to others'. |
