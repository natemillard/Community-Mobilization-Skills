## Definition

The practice of identifying, mapping, and connecting community assets — people, organizations, places, and relationships — to build a clear picture of what a community already has and who holds influence within it.

Every community is rich. Not necessarily in money or formal institutions — but in people who know things, organizations that have tried things, places where people gather, and networks that move information faster than any official channel. Asset mapping is the practice of making that richness visible.

This is not a data collection exercise. It is a relational process. The best asset maps are built through conversations, not surveys — because the most important assets are the ones that do not appear on any list.

## Examples in Red Cross Work

- A chapter team conducts a community walk and discovers three informal gathering spots that serve as neighborhood information hubs — none of which appeared in any organizational database.
- An ED maps the stakeholder landscape before a priority-setting meeting and realizes that the organizations they most rely on have overlapping boards and shared funders — creating both opportunities for alignment and potential conflicts of interest.
- A chapter team identifies a retired teacher who has lived in the neighborhood for 40 years and knows every family — and deliberately invites them into the planning process before any formal partners are engaged.

## This Skill in Everyday Life

A new neighbor who spends their first weeks asking people who they should meet, where people go, and what the unwritten rules of the neighborhood are — rather than assuming they already know — is doing asset mapping. So is anyone who draws a map of who knows whom before starting something new.

## Guiding Questions

1. What assets exist in this community that no database or report would capture?
2. Who are the connectors — the people everyone calls?
3. Whose knowledge and relationships are being treated as peripheral that should be central?
4. What would change if you started every planning process by mapping what already exists?

## Sub-Competencies

- **Diagnose the current state of local assets** — Identify and categorize community assets across people, organizations, institutions, and places.
- **Design and facilitate a participatory asset-mapping session** — Run a community-led mapping process that centers resident strengths.
- **Analyze asset relationships** — Map connections between assets — networks, communication pathways, and the people who bridge different parts of the community.
- **Analyze a stakeholder landscape** — Identify key actors, their influence, and where formal and informal power sits.
- **Develop an activation plan** — Link mapped assets to specific preparedness or resilience tasks — moving from inventory to action.

## Proficiency Levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Understand It | I can explain the difference between a needs assessment and an asset map. I understand why starting with assets matters. |
| Can Do It | I have facilitated or participated in an asset-mapping process and can identify assets across at least four categories: people, organizations, places, and networks. |
| Do It Consistently | I build asset mapping into the early stages of every planning process. I regularly update my picture of who holds influence in the community. |
| Coach Others | I design asset-mapping processes for teams and community groups. I help others move from inventory to activation — connecting assets to action. |
