## Definition

The practice of designing and facilitating meetings with clearly defined outcomes, aligned agendas, and structures that ensure every gathering produces real decisions, genuine alignment, and documented next steps.

Most meetings fail before they start — because the purpose is unclear, the wrong people are in the room, and the agenda serves the organizer rather than the outcome. Purposeful meetings begin with a simple question: what needs to be different after this meeting than before it?

This skill is not about running efficient meetings. It is about designing gatherings that do real work — that surface genuine disagreement, build shared understanding, and produce decisions that people will actually follow through on.

## Examples in Red Cross Work

- An ED rewrites a standing monthly meeting agenda from information updates to decision points — and discovers in the first redesigned meeting that there are more unresolved disagreements than anyone had acknowledged.
- A chapter team tests whether the right stakeholders are in the room before finalizing a community planning agenda — and delays the meeting by two weeks to ensure a critical voice is present.
- An ED ends every meeting with a documented accountability map: who is doing what, by when, and how the group will know if it happened.

## This Skill in Everyday Life

A family that sits down on Sunday to plan the week — with a real agenda, agreements about who does what, and a check-in the following Sunday — is running a purposeful meeting. The difference between a productive family conversation and a recurring fight is often just structure.

## Guiding Questions

1. What would need to be different after this meeting for it to be worth having?
2. Who is in the room — and whose absence will make any decision made here incomplete?
3. How do you know when you have genuine agreement versus polite compliance?
4. What happens to the things that were decided in your last meeting?

## Sub-Competencies

- **Determine appropriate meeting purpose** — Decide what kind of meeting this needs to be before setting an agenda.
- **Design agendas aligned with defined outcomes** — Build an agenda where every item connects to a specific outcome.
- **Evaluate whether the right stakeholders are present** — Assess whether the people needed to accomplish the meeting's purpose are in the room.
- **Synthesize diverse inputs into shared outcomes** — Bring different perspectives into a shared problem statement and outcomes the group can commit to.
- **Document decisions, agreements, and next steps** — Ensure every meeting ends with a clear record of what was decided and who is doing what.

## Proficiency Levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Understand It | I can explain the difference between a meeting with a clear purpose and one without. I understand why agenda design matters. |
| Can Do It | I can design a meeting agenda around specific outcomes and facilitate a gathering that ends with documented next steps. |
| Do It Consistently | I test the purpose and participant list before every significant meeting. I can tell the difference between genuine agreement and polite compliance — and name it in the room. |
| Coach Others | I help others design better meetings. I model purposeful facilitation and debrief meetings to help teams understand what worked and what didn't. |
