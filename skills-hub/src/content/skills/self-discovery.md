## Definition

The reflective practice of examining your own values, assumptions, biases, and ways of showing up — foundational to all other skills. You cannot know your community until you begin to know yourself.

Self-discovery is the only skill in this set with no prerequisites — because it is the prerequisite for everything else. How you show up in a room, in a conversation, in a planning process: all of it is shaped by things you may never have examined. This skill asks you to examine them.

This is not a one-time reflection exercise. It is an ongoing practice of noticing — noticing your assumptions, your reactions, your blind spots, and the gap between your stated values and your actual behavior.

## Examples in Red Cross Work

- An ED notices they consistently give more airtime to certain voices in meetings — and realizes this reflects their own comfort with certain communication styles, not the relative importance of those voices.
- A chapter team member arrives at a community meeting with a solution already in mind and catches themselves steering the conversation toward it rather than listening.
- An ED reflects on why a partnership fell apart and recognizes that their own urgency and institutional pace was a factor — not just the community's readiness.

## This Skill in Everyday Life

A parent who notices they're parenting the way they were parented — not because it's working but because it's familiar — is doing self-discovery work. So is anyone who catches themselves reacting to a current situation based on a past wound.

## Guiding Questions

1. What do you believe about communities that you have never had anyone challenge?
2. When something goes wrong in your work, what is your first instinct about who or what is responsible?
3. What does your discomfort in certain situations tell you about your own assumptions?
4. Whose voice do you give the most weight — and why?

## Sub-Competencies

- **Examine your own values and assumptions** — Identify what you believe about communities, leadership, and change — and where those beliefs came from.
- **Recognize your own biases in real situations** — Notice when bias, urgency, or prior experience is shaping how you see or hear a community.
- **Reflect on how you show up in groups** — Evaluate your patterns of communication, listening, and participation — especially under pressure.
- **Use reflection to improve practice** — Apply self-reflection to change a specific behavior in your community work.
- **Model self-awareness for others** — Create space for others to reflect and grow by sharing your own process openly.

## Proficiency Levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Understand It | I can explain what self-discovery means in the context of community work. I understand why examining my assumptions matters. |
| Can Do It | I have engaged in structured reflection practices. I can identify at least three assumptions I hold about communities and trace where they came from. |
| Do It Consistently | I build regular reflection into my practice — after meetings, after hard conversations, after things don't go as planned. I notice my assumptions in real time. |
| Coach Others | I create space for others to reflect. I model vulnerability about my own blind spots. I design experiences that help others examine their assumptions. |
