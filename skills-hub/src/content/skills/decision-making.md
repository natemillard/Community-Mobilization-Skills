## Definition

The practice of guiding collaborative decisions and building consensus without relying on positional authority — using influence, trust, and facilitated process to move work forward across organizational and community lines.

An Executive Director is often trying to move work forward across organizational and community lines where they have no formal authority at all. The community partner is not your employee. The board member is not your direct report. The city agency operates under a different mandate entirely. And yet the work requires alignment.

This skill is about building the kind of trust, shared purpose, and process design that allows decisions to be made and followed through — not because you can compel anyone, but because you have created the conditions where moving together feels both possible and worthwhile.

## Examples in Red Cross Work

- An ED convenes a cross-sector planning group and facilitates the development of shared decision criteria before any options are presented — so that when a recommendation emerges, the group understands how they arrived there.
- A chapter team member navigates a disagreement between two community partners by creating a structured dialogue that surfaces the underlying interests behind each position, rather than trying to broker a compromise between stated positions.
- An ED builds a relationship of trust with a skeptical city official over 18 months of regular one-on-ones — with no agenda beyond understanding each other's constraints — before bringing a specific proposal.

## This Skill in Everyday Life

A neighborhood association chair who gets residents to agree on a parking policy — without being able to enforce anything, only persuade — is exercising decision-making without authority. So is anyone who has ever successfully navigated a family disagreement about where to spend the holidays.

## Guiding Questions

1. Where are you trying to move work forward without the formal authority to require it?
2. What do the people you need to align with actually care about — beyond the stated positions?
3. What conditions have to be in place for people to feel safe making a decision together?
4. When did you last use process design rather than persuasion to build consensus?

## Sub-Competencies

- **Analyze decision-making situations for authority gaps** — Identify where formal authority is limited and what influence strategies are available.
- **Diagnose stakeholder dynamics and informal power** — Map the informal power, competing interests, and alignment gaps among people whose cooperation you need.
- **Design decision processes that build trust without authority** — Create processes that build shared purpose and accountability using facilitation and relationship.
- **Evaluate the effectiveness of your own influence behaviors** — Assess whether your approach to building consensus is working.
- **Adapt leadership posture in real time under uncertainty** — Shift your approach when conditions change — sustaining momentum when conflict or ambiguity emerge.

## Proficiency Levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Understand It | I can explain the difference between positional authority and facilitative influence. I understand why process design matters for consensus-building. |
| Can Do It | I have successfully guided a group to a decision without relying on my formal role. I can map stakeholder interests and design a dialogue that surfaces them. |
| Do It Consistently | I regularly build relationships before I need them. I design decision processes rather than just convening people and hoping for alignment. |
| Coach Others | I help others understand influence dynamics and design processes for consensus. I model facilitative leadership and debrief collaborative decision-making experiences. |
