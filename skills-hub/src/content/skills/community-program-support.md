## Definition

The practice of intentionally shifting program ownership to community stakeholders through phased transition, leadership development, and sustainable capacity building — so communities can sustain and lead their own resilience.

The question that this skill begins with is the hardest one in community mobilization: what are we building toward? If the answer is a community that relies on the Red Cross indefinitely, the work has not succeeded. If the answer is a community that has the leadership, relationships, and systems to sustain its own resilience — with the Red Cross as a resource rather than a manager — that is what this skill is designed to build toward.

Community Program Support is not about exiting. It is about the intentional, phased shift of ownership — doing more with community partners, doing less on their behalf, until the community is holding the work and the organization is alongside rather than in front.

## Examples in Red Cross Work

- A chapter team creates a two-year transition plan for a Sound the Alarm program — mapping community leaders who could own each component, identifying training and support each would need, and designing a phase-down of chapter operational control with clear milestones.
- An ED shifts the convening role in a community coalition to a resident-led steering committee — and deliberately takes a seat at the table rather than the head of it.
- A chapter team documents everything they know about how a program works — the relationships, the informal knowledge, the community context — not in a policy manual but in a series of structured conversations with the community members who will eventually run it.

## This Skill in Everyday Life

A parent who deliberately gives their teenager increasing responsibility for managing their own schedule, making their own decisions, and living with the consequences — while remaining available but not controlling — is practicing community program support. The goal is a capable adult, not a compliant child.

## Guiding Questions

1. Which of your current programs could a community partner run as well as or better than you — and what would it take to get there?
2. What are you holding on to that the community is ready to lead?
3. What does your chapter's role look like when it is working well — and what does it look like when it is working too hard?
4. How would you know when a transition was genuinely complete?

## Sub-Competencies

- **Analyze the current level of community ownership in a program** — Assess who currently holds decision-making authority and where gaps to full community ownership are.
- **Evaluate power structures within a program** — Identify who holds formal and informal authority and where increased community leadership is possible.
- **Design a phased transition plan** — Create a clear, phased plan for shifting ownership from the organization to community stakeholders.
- **Build community capacity and leadership systems** — Develop the skills, relationships, and organizational infrastructure community members need to sustain the program independently.
- **Adapt leadership approach to support community-led implementation** — Shift from leading the work to facilitating shared decision-making.

## Proficiency Levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Understand It | I can explain why shifting ownership to the community is both the goal and the challenge of community mobilization work. |
| Can Do It | I have mapped a program for potential community ownership and identified the gaps in capacity, relationships, and systems that would need to be filled. |
| Do It Consistently | I build ownership transfer into program design from the beginning, not as an afterthought. I track community leadership development as a program outcome. |
| Coach Others | I help teams design transition plans and have difficult conversations about what holding on vs. letting go looks like in practice. I model stepping back. |
