## Definition

The practice of building genuine trust through intentional one-on-one conversations and deep listening to lived experience — creating the relational foundation that makes all community work possible.

Trust is not a step in the community mobilization process. Trust *is* the process. Everything else — partnerships, planning, shared decision-making — depends on the quality of the relationships beneath it. This skill is about building those relationships intentionally, one conversation at a time.

## Examples in Red Cross Work

- An ED schedules one-on-one meetings with community leaders before any program planning begins — not to pitch, but to listen and learn.
- A chapter team member sits with disaster survivors to understand their experience of recovery — what helped, what failed, what was missing — before designing the next phase of support.
- An ED revisits a community partner whose engagement has dropped off, not to ask what went wrong but to understand what has changed for them and what they need now.

## This Skill in Everyday Life

A parent who sits down with a struggling teenager — not to offer advice or solutions but to ask what is going on and genuinely listen — is practicing trust-building. A friend who calls to check in after a hard week, not to fix anything but to be present, is practicing trust-building. This skill lives far outside formal community work.

## Guiding Questions

1. Who in this community do I have a genuine relationship with — and who am I only connected to transactionally?
2. When was the last time I listened to someone without thinking about what I would say next?
3. Whose lived experience is shaping the decisions I am making — and whose is not?
4. What would it mean to build trust slowly rather than assuming it because of my role?

## Sub-Competencies

- **Diagnose motivations, values, and self-interests in conversation** — What matters most to this person? What do they want from this relationship?
- **Design and conduct an intentional one-on-one conversation** — Structure: purpose, exploration, exchange, clear next step.
- **Create safe space for lived experience to be shared** — No agenda. No interpretation. Center the other person's reality.
- **Adjust communication in real time to reduce bias** — Notice when your assumptions are affecting what you hear.
- **Integrate relational conversations into ongoing mobilization** — Use 1:1 conversations to build durable partnerships over time.

## Proficiency Levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Understand It | I can explain what a relational conversation is and why it matters. I understand the difference between a transactional meeting and a trust-building conversation. |
| Can Do It | I have conducted intentional one-on-ones and can structure them around listening rather than information exchange. I can hold space for lived experience to be shared. |
| Do It Consistently | I regularly schedule relational conversations before program planning, not after. I notice in real time when a conversation is becoming transactional and can shift it. |
| Coach Others | I can help colleagues prepare for and debrief relational conversations. I model deep listening in team settings and create conditions for others to practice this skill. |
