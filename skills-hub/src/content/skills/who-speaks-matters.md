## Definition

The practice of recognizing and actively responding to power dynamics in group settings — ensuring that all voices, especially those historically excluded, have genuine influence in decisions and dialogue.

Every room has a power dynamic. People hold formal authority — titles, roles, control over resources. People hold informal authority — trust, relationships, credibility within a community. And some people have neither — and have learned to stay quiet in rooms where the decision will be made without them anyway.

This skill is about seeing those dynamics clearly and doing something about them. Not just creating opportunities for participation, but actively redistributing voice and influence in ways that change who gets to shape the outcome.

## Examples in Red Cross Work

- An ED facilitating a community planning meeting notices that the same three people have spoken in every discussion and pauses to specifically invite quieter voices before moving to decision-making.
- A chapter team recognizes that a long-term partner organization has been dominating the agenda-setting process and restructures the planning group to include direct community representation.
- An ED designs a meeting agenda that puts community voice before organizational presentations — so that organizational perspectives respond to community realities, not the other way around.

## This Skill in Everyday Life

A teacher who calls on students who rarely raise their hands — not to embarrass them but because they know those students have something to contribute — is practicing this skill. So is a family member who asks to hear from the youngest person in the room before the family makes a decision.

## Guiding Questions

1. Who speaks most in the meetings you facilitate — and what does that tell you?
2. Who is present but silent — and what might be making it hard for them to speak?
3. What decisions have been made in the name of this community without their input?
4. How would you know if a facilitation process was genuinely equitable?

## Sub-Competencies

- **Define power-aware engagement** — Explain what formal and informal power are and how they shape community dynamics.
- **Analyze how power shapes engagement** — Identify where power is operating in a given group — who is centered, who is marginalized.
- **Recognize and address power dynamics in real time** — Notice power imbalances as they arise and respond with intentional facilitation strategies.
- **Facilitate discussions that increase underrepresented participation** — Use specific techniques to invite contributions from quieter or historically excluded participants.
- **Design meeting structures that distribute participation** — Create agendas and processes that prevent dominance from the start.

## Proficiency Levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Understand It | I can explain what power-aware engagement means and give examples of formal and informal power in community settings. |
| Can Do It | I can identify power dynamics in a room I am facilitating and use at least two techniques to redistribute participation. |
| Do It Consistently | I design meeting structures with equity in mind from the start — not just as a correction after dominance emerges. |
| Coach Others | I help others see power dynamics they are not noticing. I debrief facilitation experiences with a power lens. |
