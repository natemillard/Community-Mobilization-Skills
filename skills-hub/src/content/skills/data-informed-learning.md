## Definition

The practice of designing repeatable systems to collect field data, interpret patterns, and translate learnings into concrete action — creating a continuous cycle of improvement grounded in evidence.

Data-informed learning is not about having the most data. It is about having the right questions and building simple, sustainable systems to answer them. Most community organizations are awash in data they never use — and making decisions based on intuition about things they could actually measure.

This skill asks: what do we need to know, how will we find out, and what will we change when we learn something new? The answer to that last question is the one most often skipped.

## Examples in Red Cross Work

- A chapter uses a simple monthly tracking sheet to notice that a particular neighborhood has significantly lower enrollment in their programs than neighboring areas — and discovers through conversation that trust, not awareness, is the barrier.
- An ED presents program data to a community advisory group and asks them to interpret it — rather than arriving with their own interpretation — and learns something they would not have found on their own.
- A chapter team designs a simple three-question feedback form distributed at every event, reads it together monthly, and changes something based on it every quarter.

## This Skill in Everyday Life

A coach who tracks game statistics not to rank players but to identify patterns — which formations work, which opponents expose weaknesses — and adjusts practice accordingly is practicing data-informed learning. So is a gardener who keeps a journal of what thrives and what fails each season.

## Guiding Questions

1. What decisions are you currently making based on intuition that you could actually measure?
2. When you collect feedback, do you change anything based on it — and does the community know you did?
3. What data would surprise you — and what does that tell you about your assumptions?
4. What is the simplest feedback system you could sustain consistently?

## Sub-Competencies

- **Identify key program data sources** — Name the metrics, indicators, and data sources relevant to program outcomes.
- **Assess data quality and limitations** — Evaluate whether data is complete, reliable, and unbiased before drawing conclusions.
- **Analyze field data to identify patterns and gaps** — Look across data sources to identify meaningful patterns and unintended consequences.
- **Design and implement a feedback loop** — Create a simple system that links field learnings to measurable indicators and documented action changes.
- **Adapt program strategy based on learnings** — Change what you are doing based on what you are learning — and communicate the rationale clearly.

## Proficiency Levels

| Level | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Understand It | I can explain what a feedback loop is and why data-informed decision-making matters more than intuition alone. |
| Can Do It | I have designed or used a simple data collection system and can interpret basic patterns from program data. |
| Do It Consistently | I have a functioning feedback loop that regularly produces learnings I act on. I communicate changes back to the community so they know their input mattered. |
| Coach Others | I help teams design learning systems. I facilitate data interpretation sessions that invite community voice into the analysis. |
